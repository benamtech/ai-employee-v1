# Competitive Landscape & AMTECH Whitespace

**Status: complete** · _Revised 2026-06-27: added the intelligence-curve differentiator and contractor-tuned call language._

## Competitive tiers

| Tier | Examples | What they are | Why they are not the AMTECH wedge |
|---|---|---|---|
| Toy AI assistants | Sintra-style AI helpers | Cheap helpers, templates, broad tasks. Sintra pricing showed promotional plans around $15.60-$48.50/month [S034]. | Low price, but not a done-for-you vertical workflow sold around an operator's urgent task. |
| DIY agent builders | Relevance AI, Lindy-style builders | Flexible platforms for building agents/workflows. Relevance positions around AI workforce scaling [S033]. | The owner still has to design, configure, maintain, and debug the workflow. |
| Single-function AI workers | Artisan/Ava, 11x-style sales agents | Done-for-you or semi-managed workers for outbound/sales use cases; third-party pricing estimates are often several thousand/month [S035]. | Narrow function, usually outbound-heavy, usually too expensive/enterprise for AMTECH's first SMB sale. |
| Automation/AI agencies | Custom builds and retainers | Consultants build workflows, integrations, or bots. | Often bespoke, slow, and can create maintenance burden. AMTECH should avoid looking like this. |
| RAG/chatbot vendors | Custom GPT/chatbot tools | Customer-facing Q&A over docs/sites. | Different lane. AMTECH is an internal back-office worker, not a customer-facing chatbot. |
| Human substitutes | VA, coordinator, CSR, assistant PM, bookkeeper | The real budget anchor. | AMTECH must sell below the cost/friction of another human while still feeling managed. |

## AMTECH whitespace

AMTECH's strongest whitespace is not "AI employee" as a phrase. Competitors already use similar language [S034]. The whitespace is:

**Done-for-you, vertical-task-first, owner-supervised internal office labor that starts as a low-trust Skill and graduates into a managed AI Employee.**

That is different from cheap helper apps, DIY builders, sales-only AI workers, and custom agencies. The Skill gives Ben a low-ticket entry product; the Employee captures the management pain after the owner sees the value but does not want to keep running the workflow manually.

### The structural differentiator: AMTECH rides the intelligence curve

The **software** a contractor normally buys is static from the buyer's seat: it's as good as it will ever be the day he installs it (and often just gets more bloated), a DIY agent only improves if the owner re-engineers it, and a single-function vendor is locked to one job. AMTECH is different. Because the deliverable is produced by the model + agent scaffolding, **every installed Employee's work quality rises as the underlying intelligence improves — at the same price, with no effort from the owner.** AMTECH manages the model swaps and absorbs model drift so the owner always has the current best intelligence packaged into their worker. The moat is not the intelligence (that commoditizes); it is the packaging, the loaded business context, the trust relationship, and being further up the office stack than any self-serve tool. For an AI-naive contractor, that gap — between "frontier capability exists" and "it reliably runs my estimates" — is permanent.

## Failure-stat validation

- RAND reports that some estimates put AI project failure above 80%, roughly twice the rate for non-AI IT projects [S036].
- NBER's business-use evidence says more than 90% report no employment effect and 89% report no labor-productivity impact [S037].
- The MIT/NANDA "GenAI Divide" report surfaced in search says 95% of organizations get zero return from GenAI pilots [S038].

Call implication: do not say "AI is magic." Say generic AI fails because the owner still has to adapt it to the business. AMTECH sells the adapted workflow and the managed worker.

## How to say the difference on a call

- **What AMTECH is:** "A done-for-you tool that writes your estimates first, then an always-on employee that does it off a voice memo if you want it handled without prompting."
- **What it is not:** "It is not a chatbot for your website and not a lead-gen spam machine — it does *your* office work, behind your approval."
- **Compared to ChatGPT:** "ChatGPT waits for you to know what to ask and feed it everything every time; this already knows your pricing and format and writes *your* estimate — within about 5%."
- **Compared to DIY agent builders:** "Those hand you tools to build and babysit an assistant yourself; AMTECH gives you the finished one and keeps it running."
- **Compared to AI agencies:** "This starts with one job and a fixed price — $300 to tune your estimate — not an open-ended consulting project."
- **Compared to VAs/admin/estimator hires:** "An office person is three to five grand a month, plus hiring, training, and managing them. This starts at $300 and writes the estimate the way you would."
- **The thing none of them can say (the closer):** "And it gets *better on its own* as the AI improves — without you doing anything or paying more. Most tools you buy are as good as they'll ever be the day you get them; this is the opposite. That's why you want it installed and learning your business now."

**Positioning guardrail (carry into every call and page):** never pitch this as beating or replacing people — the buyer is a person who employs people, and the product is owner-supervised by design. The frame is **time back**: it does the repetitive grind (estimates at 9pm) so the owner and his crew spend their hours on the craft, the customers, and the work only a person can do, with the owner approving everything. The "it improves on its own" claim is a comparison to **static software**, not to humans. Humans get better too — that is not the point, and saying otherwise is both false and off-brand.
